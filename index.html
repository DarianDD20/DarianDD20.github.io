about.md

    

    

      

        I'm a software engineer who likes problems with more than one layer to them: a vision
        pipeline that has to survive bad lighting and a cluttered background, a model that has
        to reason about motion over time instead of a single frame, a game loop that has to feel
        responsive at sixty frames a second. I tend to gravitate toward projects where the
        "obvious" approach breaks immediately and the real work is in the ablation — finding
        out, methodically, what actually moves the needle.
      

      

        That shows up across what I build: deep learning pipelines for action recognition and
        currency classification, a point-of-sale system built for a real small business, and a
        C++ dungeon crawler built from a blank SFML window up to a full game loop with menus,
        dialogue, and cinematics. Different domains, same instinct — break the system into
        phases, measure each one, and don't trust a result you can't explain.

            A scale- and rotation-invariant computer vision pipeline that classifies South African
            bank notes against cluttered, highly textured backgrounds. Rather than picking an
            architecture by instinct, I built a custom sequential optimization framework that
            benchmarked candidate algorithms through a data-isolated ablation study across three
            phases: preprocessing (CLAHE, bilateral filtering, Gaussian blur for contrast and
            noise), segmentation (boundary isolation and geometric normalization, with strict
            protection against color-data leakage), and classification (SIFT with FLANN, ORB,
            AKAZE, and 3D HSV histograms compared head-to-head).
